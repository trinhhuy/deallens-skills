# Investor Lens

When user context indicates investor due diligence (angel, VC, M&A evaluator), adapt the brief with these adjustments.

## Sections to emphasize

### Replace "Overview" with "Business model & unit economics"

Beyond what they do, capture HOW they make money:
- Pricing model (subscription, transaction, ads, hybrid)
- Pricing tiers if public
- ACV/ARPU estimates if disclosed
- Customer mix (enterprise vs SMB vs prosumer)
- Geographic distribution

Note where data is missing — gaps matter for an investor.

### Add "Traction signals" section

Focus on growth metrics:
- Revenue numbers (if disclosed in press, podcasts, blog posts)
- Customer count or logo growth
- Headcount growth trajectory (2x year-over-year? Flat?)
- Geographic expansion
- Product launch cadence
- Hiring patterns (which roles? Senior or junior weighted?)

### Add "Competitive positioning" section

- Direct competitors (3-5)
- Their differentiation claim
- Realistic assessment: is the moat real?
- Market dynamics (consolidating? Fragmenting? Mature?)

### Add "Founder & team" with depth

For founders:
- Background (prior companies, exits, education)
- Public thought leadership (podcast appearances, essays, talks)
- Track record indicators
- Public signal of founder-market fit

For broader team:
- Quality of recent senior hires
- Advisory board if listed
- Notable engineers/designers (signals product quality)

### Add "Risk factors" section

Be honest and thorough:
- Market risks (declining market? Regulatory threats?)
- Competitive risks (well-funded incumbents?)
- Team risks (single point of failure?)
- Product risks (commoditization? Platform dependency?)
- Financial signals (high burn rate from job postings? Recent layoffs?)

### Add "Key questions for diligence" section

5-10 questions an investor should investigate further:
- Questions for the founder
- Questions to ask references
- Data to request (cohort retention, NRR, gross margins)
- Customers to call

## Tone adjustments

- Analytical, not promotional
- Use investor language: TAM, NRR, payback period, moat, GTM
- Highlight what's missing or unverifiable
- Distinguish "fact" from "claim" — companies make claims, facts come from third parties

## Example transformation

Original: "🟢 **Funding** — Oct 2025. Closed $50M Series C led by Sequoia."

Investor lens: "🟢 **Funding** — Oct 2025. $50M Series C led by Sequoia at reported $400M post-money (8x revenue multiple per Bloomberg). **Investor view**: Strong signal (Sequoia diligence quality), but multiple is rich for the segment — implies aggressive growth expectations. Investigate: revenue growth rate, NRR, and burn multiple to assess if multiple is justified."

## What investors care about that others don't

- **Capital efficiency**: ratio of revenue to capital raised
- **Burn rate signals**: aggressive hiring = high burn
- **Time to liquidity**: realistic exit paths
- **Cap table dynamics**: who's diluted, who has preference
- **Comparable transactions**: similar companies, what did they exit at

If user is specifically evaluating for investment, dig into these even if data is limited and explicitly flag what data is needed to complete diligence.
